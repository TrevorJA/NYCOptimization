## 1. Introduction

Water supply systems worldwide face growing stress from hydroclimatic change. Many of their operating rules were negotiated decades ago under the assumption of a stationary hydrology and are increasingly required to perform under conditions outside their design basis. Many-objective evolutionary search coupled with simulation has become a standard tool for exploring how such rules could be adapted, and for revealing the tradeoffs among competing water uses that any renegotiation must confront (Reed et al., 2013; Giuliani et al., 2016; Herman et al., 2020). A defining feature of this approach is that every candidate policy is evaluated over a finite set of streamflow scenarios, thousands to millions of times during a single search. The composition of that scenario set determines which hydrologic conditions are represented during search, and therefore which vulnerabilities the resulting policies are shaped to manage. In most applications, however, the search ensemble is inherited rather than designed. The two most common choices are the historical record itself and an independent sample from a fitted stochastic generator.

The evidence that this choice matters is growing but two-sided. Cohen et al. (2021), the nearest antecedent to the present study, framed reservoir policy search as a training and testing problem and showed that the properties of the training scenarios determine out-of-sample robustness. In their experiment, policies trained on scenario subsets with high baseline regret matched the performance of policies trained on all scenarios at approximately one third of the computational cost, whereas subsets defined by coarse hydrologic aggregates failed to identify the valuable scenarios. Zaniolo et al. (2023) showed that the drought type embedded in a training ensemble changes not only performance but also the installed infrastructure, the technology mix, and the state variables that a policy consults. Brodeur et al. (2020) demonstrated that policies calibrated to a single hydrologic sequence overfit to its event sequencing and that resampled training sets generalize better. The strongest available null result nevertheless cautions against assuming that any designed selection outperforms sampling. Eker and Kwakkel (2018) found that scenarios selected for policy relevance and diversity "[do] not lead to significantly more favorable results in terms of robustness or variety of robustness tradeoffs, compared to an arbitrary set of scenarios." In addition, Giuliani and Castelletti (2016) showed that policies designed under one across-scenario aggregation and scored under another are systematically dominated by policies designed under the scoring measure, which is a direct warning to any method that deliberately alters the scenario distribution during search. Whether a designed search ensemble can outperform a probabilistic one is therefore a genuinely open question rather than a foregone conclusion, and answering it requires an experiment built to detect the effect if it exists and to report its absence if it does not.

We approach that question by distinguishing three spaces that the literature often conflates. The input space contains the factors that define scenario generation, such as generator parameters, climate multipliers, and demand factors. A sampled point in this space is a state of the world (SOW) in the terminology of the robust-decision-making lineage (Kasprzyk et al., 2013; Trindade et al., 2017). The outcome space contains simulation outputs, which are the objective values of a candidate policy under a scenario. Between them sits what we term the hazard space, defined as the space of hydrologic hazard metrics computed directly on each realized streamflow sequence before any system simulation. Examples of hazard coordinates include drought event severity, duration, and intensity, together with low-flow and high-flow indices. Hazard coordinates are properties of the scenario rather than of the simulated system response, and they are computable for millions of candidate sequences at negligible cost. The closest existing construct is the exposure space of the scenario-neutral tradition (Culley et al., 2016; Fowler et al., 2024). Exposure spaces, however, are typically attribute targets imposed on generator inputs, whereas hazard coordinates are measured on realized output sequences.

The motivating observation is that neither of the prevailing search ensembles is designed to represent the hydrologic conditions that stress a system. The historical record contains one realization of natural variability, so its hazard content is whatever the observed decades happened to deliver. An independent sample from a stochastic generator reproduces the frequency of hazard conditions but, precisely because it is probability-faithful, it populates the common center of the hazard distribution densely and the severe corners sparsely, even though the severe corners are where reservoir policies are decided. Prior work has controlled hydrologic hazard in a search ensemble only in restricted ways. Zatarain Salazar et al. (2017) stratified a stationary synthetic pool on a single realized-flow statistic during search, in one dimension and preserving the parent probabilities. Herman et al. (2016) and Zaniolo et al. (2023, 2024) control drought properties at generation time, at a small number of discrete, analyst-chosen levels. Bonham et al. (2024) introduced space-filling subsampling of state-of-the-world ensembles to water resources, but for post-hoc robustness ranking rather than for the search ensemble. Bartholomew and Kwakkel (2020) close their comparison of robustness-in-search methods with an open problem that this study takes up directly: "Research is needed into the selection [of] scenarios which as a set contain the appropriately stressing conditions against which solutions have to be robust, while also capturing the scenarios under which one would like to have near optimal performance."

This study contributes hazard filling, a search-ensemble construction that selects scenarios from a large candidate pool of synthetic streamflow sequences so that the retained members cover the hydrologic hazard space, deliberately over-representing the severe corners relative to their frequency under the generating process. The selection is simulation-free, because hazard coordinates are computed on the sequences alone, so the design scales to candidate pools of 100,000 to 1,000,000 members and transfers across objective formulations. Problem-driven selection properties such as baseline regret, by contrast, require one perfect-foresight optimization per candidate scenario (Cohen et al., 2021). The selector is deliberately the simplest defensible space-filling design, consisting of Latin hypercube anchors in the hazard space that are each snapped to the nearest unused pool member. We test this construction against the discipline's default, an independent and identically distributed sample from the same stochastic generator, in a controlled comparison at matched ensemble size, record length, and function-evaluation budget, on a real multi-reservoir system. The application is the re-optimization of New York City's Delaware River Basin reservoir operations within the Flexible Flow Management Program (FFMP) rule structure, simulated with the open-source Pywr-DRB model (Hamilton et al., 2024).

Because a design that deliberately alters the scenario distribution is not commensurable with a probabilistic one on its own search objectives, all cross-design comparison is made in a single way. Every design's Pareto-approximate policies are re-evaluated on one common, held-out test ensemble, and compared there with a fixed set of robustness metrics (Zatarain Salazar et al., 2017; McPhail et al., 2020). The test ensemble is constructed to be deeply uncertain: a Latin hypercube over a wide range of climate-forcing perturbations, crossed with stochastic realizations. Because the search ensembles are drawn from the unperturbed generator while the test ensemble spans a forced climate envelope, the comparison measures whether hazard-space coverage of the natural-variability manifold produces policies that generalize to conditions never presented during search. This is a stronger test than re-evaluating on the same distribution used to search, and it keeps the test instrument structurally distinct from both search ensembles.

To our knowledge, this is the first study to test hazard-space scenario selection against probabilistic sampling at matched ensemble size, sequence length, and search budget on a real system, with both designs re-evaluated on a common held-out, deeply uncertain ensemble and with a replication scheme that separates ensemble-construction variance from search variance. Three research questions structure the analysis.

1. Does constructing the search ensemble by hazard-space coverage, rather than by independent sampling from the same stochastic generator, change the robustness of the resulting Pareto-approximate policies under held-out, deeply uncertain re-evaluation? (RQ1, the core question.)
2. Can re-optimizing the parameters of the existing FFMP rule structure improve NYC and basin-wide outcomes, including water supply reliability, Montague and Trenton flow-target compliance, downstream flood exposure, and storage resilience, relative to current operations? (RQ2.)
3. Does a variable-resolution FFMP structure with more storage zones improve performance or robustness relative to the current zone structure? (RQ3.)

## 2. The Delaware River Basin Test Case

### 2.1 The NYC reservoir system and the Flexible Flow Management Program

The Delaware River Basin (DRB) spans New York, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Delaware, and supplies approximately one half of New York City's drinking water through three upper-basin reservoirs (Cannonsville, Pepacton, and Neversink) whose diversions pass through the Rondout balancing reservoir and the Delaware Aqueduct. Basin operations are governed by the 1954 U.S. Supreme Court Decree in *New Jersey v. New York*, which caps NYC diversions at 800 MGD, entitles New Jersey to 100 MGD, and obligates NYC to make compensating releases that maintain a minimum flow of 1,750 cfs (1,131 MGD) at Montague, NJ. A companion flow objective of 3,000 cfs (1,939 MGD) at Trenton, NJ, protects downstream ecosystems and repels the salt front from the Philadelphia and Camden water intakes. Changes to the operation of NYC's Delaware reservoirs require unanimous consent of the five Decree parties.

Since 2007, releases have been managed under successive versions of the Flexible Flow Management Program, most recently the 2017 FFMP. The FFMP is a staged, storage-indexed rule structure. Combined NYC storage is compared daily against seasonally varying threshold curves that define operating zones ranging from flood-mitigation levels through normal operations to three drought stages. Each zone prescribes diversion curtailments for NYC and NJ and scaling factors on each reservoir's minimum release, modulated by seasonal release profiles, and additional flood-mitigation releases apply above the highest storage zones. The central operational tension is structural. During severe drought, NYC's authorized diversion, the Montague compensating releases, and downstream ecological flows cannot all be met simultaneously, while wet-period storage management trades flood mitigation against drought reserve. This governance structure supplies a natural many-objective formulation. The FFMP's parameterized rules, which consist of negotiated quantities such as release baselines, drought curtailment factors, and storage-zone boundaries, supply a decision space whose re-optimization is institutionally meaningful because the rule structure is preserved and only its negotiated constants are varied (Section 3.1).

### 2.2 Simulation model

All policy evaluations use Pywr-DRB, an open-source daily water-availability and drought-risk model of the basin built on the Pywr simulation framework (Hamilton et al., 2024). Pywr-DRB represents the four NYC reservoirs and the major lower-basin reservoirs, the FFMP rule structure with its storage zones, seasonal release profiles, drought curtailments, and flood-mitigation releases, the Montague and Trenton flow targets with their time-of-travel lags, and NYC and NJ deliveries under the Decree's running-average accounting.

During optimization we use a trimmed configuration of the model in which the non-NYC reservoirs are simulated once under their default rules and their releases are supplied to the trimmed model as fixed boundary inflows. Each function evaluation therefore solves only the NYC-relevant portion of the network. This simplification is admissible because the decision variables (Section 3.1) alter only NYC operations, and the lower-basin reservoirs' rules do not respond to NYC decisions in the model. The full model is retained wherever absolute fidelity matters. It is used to generate the boundary releases, to simulate the status-quo baseline policy, and for all held-out re-evaluation (Section 6). Verification that the trimmed model reproduces full-model objective values under matched inputs is reported in Supporting Information Section S1.

## 3. Problem Formulation

### 3.1 Decision variables

The decision vector parameterizes the 2017 FFMP rule structure with 24 variables. These comprise the five minimum-flow-target baselines (the Cannonsville, Pepacton, and Neversink release targets and the Montague and Trenton flow targets), the maximum NYC delivery, NYC diversion curtailment factors for the three drought stages, NJ curtailment factors for the two deepest stages, six additive vertical shifts to the seasonally varying storage-zone threshold curves, the three reservoirs' maximum flood-mitigation release rates, and four seasonal scaling factors on the daily minimum-release profiles. Bounds bracket the negotiated 2017 FFMP values, which constitute the status-quo policy against which improvement is measured. The FFMP's structural logic, including its storage-indexed stages, seasonal profiles, and running-average delivery accounting, is preserved exactly.

### 3.2 Objectives

The objective set spans the stakeholder priorities that the basin's governance encodes: NYC supply, the Decree flow obligations protecting New Jersey and Philadelphia, downstream flood exposure, and reservoir-system resilience. Seven objectives are active (Table 2). An eighth objective, NJ delivery reliability, will be added only if a redundancy screen shows that it is not collinear with Trenton flow reliability. [Open item: the eighth objective is pending the objective-sensitivity experiment.] All metrics are computed on post-warm-up daily output, with the first 365 days of each simulation excluded, and with weekly accounting following the Decree convention. Compliance is always scored against the static 1954 Decree quantities of 800 MGD for NYC, 1,131.05 MGD at Montague, 1,938.95 MGD at Trenton, and 100 MGD for NJ, and never against the FFMP's live drought-adjusted targets. Scoring against the live target would allow a policy to satisfy its obligations by triggering the drought step-downs that lower those obligations. For NYC and NJ supply the Decree limits the running *average* of diversion rather than any single day's, so daily demand is not clipped at the static right; instead each day's delivery target is the realizable entitlement — the smaller of that day's demand and the running-average allowance banked to it under the FFMP delivery accounting — reconstructed at the static right so that a policy's own drought step-downs cannot lower it. A demand spike above the flat daily right is therefore honored whenever prior under-use has banked the allowance for it, while demand beyond the banked right is not counted as owed.

Two formulation choices depart from common practice deliberately, following the objective-stability findings of Quinn et al. (2017) and the convergence findings of Bonham et al. (2024). First, tail objectives use the conditional value-at-risk at the 90 percent level (CVaR90), defined as the mean of the worst decile of weekly deficits (Rockafellar and Uryasev, 2000; Löhndorf, 2016; Fairbrother et al., 2022), rather than the single worst week, which Quinn et al. (2017) identify as a high-variance, low-information signal. Second, flood exposure is a count of days above the National Weather Service minor flood stage at three vulnerable gauges (Hale Eddy, Fishs Eddy, and Bridgeville). This count-over-threshold form avoids the known pathologies of expected-damage objectives (Quinn et al., 2017). Storage resilience uses the fifth percentile of aggregate NYC storage rather than the single-day minimum. Trenton flow reliability stands in for salt-front position because the Trenton target exists to repel the salt front, which makes the two physically redundant, and because the flow form avoids reliance on a statistical salinity emulator in the extreme droughts where objective values are decided.

### 3.3 Across-realization aggregation during search

For the single-trace historic design the temporal metrics of Table 2 are the objectives. Each ensemble design must additionally collapse a matrix of objective values across realizations to one scalar per objective at each function evaluation. We use a two-layer annual-unit scheme. Each realization's post-warm-up series is divided into water-year units. The first stage computes each objective's annual metric on every realization-year unit, and the second stage aggregates across the pooled *N*(*L*−1) unit-years with a per-objective unit operator,

$$
J_i \;=\; \Psi_i\big[\,\{\varphi_i(r, y)\}_{r=1..N,\; y=1..L-1}\,\big],
$$

where $\varphi_i$ is objective *i*'s annual metric and $\Psi_i$ is its unit operator. The reliability objectives (1, 3, 5, and 8) use an annual failure indicator, which registers a failure when at least one week in the unit-year violates the corresponding Decree goalpost, and take $\Psi$ as the frequency of non-failure years. This satisficing-frequency form is the search-time reliability operator used throughout the regional water-supply lineage (Zeff et al., 2014; Trindade et al., 2017; Gold et al., 2023). The deficit and storage objectives (2, 4, and 7) take $\Psi$ as the worst first-percentile unit-year, the stable tail form of Quinn et al. (2017, 2018). The flood-days objective (6) takes $\Psi$ as the mean across unit-years, which corresponds to expected annual flood days. A 99th-percentile variant is registered because expectations can mask floods (Quinn et al., 2017). [Open item: the flood-days operator choice is pending the ensemble objective-sensitivity experiment.] The historic record is scored as consecutive annual units with inherited state, following Quinn et al. (2018), so it carries the same unit denominator per realization-year as the ensemble designs. Unit-years within a realization are serially dependent, since consecutive failure-years are precisely how multi-year droughts express themselves in this scheme. The effective sample size is therefore below *N*(*L*−1), which is disclosed rather than corrected (Section 8). At the campaign size (*N* = 100, *L* = 10) the pooled denominator is 900 unit-years, comparable to the 1,000 one-year members that Quinn et al. (2017, 2018) use to stabilize the same worst-first-percentile operator, which is the ground for a per-evaluation ensemble smaller than the 1,000-realization convention of the Zeff–Herman–Trindade lineage (Section 5.1).

Two properties of this scheme matter for the comparison. First, the temporal metrics of Table 2 and the entire held-out re-evaluation metric set (Section 6) are identical across designs. Only this across-realization stage differs, and it differs only because the two ensemble designs present different scenario compositions to it. Second, cross-design commensurability during search is neither required nor claimed. A design that deliberately over-represents severe hazard conditions computes search objectives under a distribution that differs from the generating process (Section 4.5), and the held-out re-evaluation is the only comparison point.

## 4. Scenario Designs for the Search Ensemble

### 4.1 The three designs

We compare three constructions of the search ensemble, all drawn from a single stationary population and all evaluated at matched size and record length except where a design cannot be matched by construction.

The first design, `historic`, is the observed record simulated as one continuous trace, with one realization of length 77 years. It is the reference for prevailing applied practice (Giuliani et al., 2016; Herman et al., 2020). It cannot be matched in ensemble size or budget and is reported as a reference rather than entered into the controlled contrast.

The second design, `fixed_probabilistic`, consists of *N* realizations of length *L* drawn independently and identically from the stationary stochastic generator and frozen for the entire search. This is the standard construction of the regional water-supply lineage (Quinn et al., 2017; Zatarain Salazar et al., 2017) and the reference against which designed selection is judged. It is the discipline's default, and the contrast between it and the third design is the core of RQ1.

The third design, `hazard_filling`, is the proposed method. It selects *N* realizations from a large candidate pool by space-filling coverage of the hydrologic hazard space, using the selector of Section 4.4. It is constructed from the same stationary generator as `fixed_probabilistic`, at the same *N* and *L*, so that the two designs differ only in the rule that selects which realizations enter the search ensemble.

The stationary population is the flow model of the prevailing water-supply optimization lineage (Herman et al., 2014; Trindade et al., 2017; Zatarain Salazar et al., 2017; Gold et al., 2023). Hazard variation within it arises entirely from natural variability, in that some 10-year windows contain severe multi-year droughts and most do not. Restricting the search to this population is deliberate. It isolates the question of RQ1, which is whether the rule that selects realizations from a fixed generating process matters, without confounding that rule with a change in the generating process itself. The deeply uncertain climate-forcing space enters the study only in the held-out test ensemble (Section 6.1), where it makes the re-evaluation a test of generalization to conditions absent from search. Prior work supports hazard-oriented selection within a stationary population: Zatarain Salazar et al. (2017) stratify a stationary pool by a realized-flow statistic, and Herman et al. (2016) amplify drought severity with a stationary weighted bootstrap.

### 4.2 The generator and the candidate pool

Daily multi-site realizations are generated by the Kirsch–Nowak pipeline. Cholesky-based monthly generation preserves cross-site and lag correlations (Kirsch et al., 2013), and a nearest-neighbour resampling scheme disaggregates monthly flows to daily flows (Nowak et al., 2010). The generator is fitted to the reconstructed observed record (1945–2022) and run without climate perturbation.

Both ensemble designs draw from this one generator, and the distinction between them is the mechanism of selection. Because the generator's parameters are a knob, a design can generate as many independent realizations as it wants, but it cannot instruct the generator to produce a realization at a prescribed drought severity. Hazard coordinates are emergent properties of a realized sequence, computed only after the sequence exists. A design that targets hazard coverage must therefore select realizations from a finite pool of already-generated sequences, rather than generate directly to a target. The nearest-neighbour step in the selector (Section 4.4) is a consequence of this fact, not an approximation of a preferable procedure. This is a scope choice given a fixed fitted generator: a generator purpose-built to emit sequences at prescribed hazard targets is an alternative construction (Zaniolo et al., 2023, 2024), which we do not pursue because it forgoes the simulation-free scalability of selecting from a pool and requires committing to a small number of discrete target types.

The `hazard_filling` design owns a candidate pool of *P* realizations sampled independently from the stationary generator. [Open item: the pool size *P*. The production target is between 100,000 and 1,000,000 members.] The pool is never simulated. Only each realization's hazard coordinates and generation seed are stored, and the *N* selected members are regenerated exactly on demand from a deterministic, globally indexed random-stream architecture (Supporting Information Section S3). This storage arrangement is what makes pools of 100,000 to 1,000,000 members tractable.

The requirement that the candidate pool be sampled independently, rather than by a structured design such as Latin hypercube, is essential to the validity of the comparison. A uniform random subset of size *N* taken from an independent and identically distributed pool has exactly the joint distribution of *N* fresh independent draws. This distributional equivalence is what makes `fixed_probabilistic` the exact statistical control for `hazard_filling`: the two designs then differ only in the rule that selects *N* members from the same population law, and any difference in re-evaluated robustness is attributable to that rule alone. The condition is enforced by an automated invariant in the study's code, because no other component of the pipeline would fail if it were violated.

### 4.3 Realizations, windows, and hazard metrics

Every ensemble scenario is a disjoint, non-overlapping *L*-year realization. Each simulation starts from a fixed initial storage of 80 percent of capacity, and the 365-day warm-up is excluded from all metrics, which constitutes a terminating-simulation design. A drought event that would straddle a window boundary is split by it. The choice of *L* bounds this truncation (Section 5.1), and event-scale severity is additionally captured at re-evaluation, where records are at least as long.

Hazard coordinates are computed on the aggregate inflow to the three NYC reservoirs before any system simulation. The candidate axes are drought-event metrics derived from run theory applied to the six-month Standardized Streamflow Index, consisting of the deficit volume, duration, peak depth, onset rate, and recovery rate of the controlling event (McKee et al., 1993; Vicente-Serrano et al., 2012), together with flood metrics from a peaks-over-threshold analysis of daily flows, consisting of the peak magnitude, pulse duration, and rise rate. The SSI distribution fit is made once, on the observed record, and reused for every realization, so hazard coordinates are comparable across the pool and the historic reference.

Correlated axes make space-filling coverage poorly defined and inflate the effective dimension of the space. Following the index-redundancy logic of Olden and Poff (2003), the candidate pool's hazard image is screened before selection. Degenerate axes are dropped, the remaining axes are clustered on rank-correlation distance, and a balanced set spanning distinct clusters across the drought and flood concepts is retained. The target is a set of three to four axes, which is large enough to separate drought from flood behavior and small enough that *N* = 100 points can fill the space (Section 5.1). ### 4.4 The hazard-filling selector

The selector fills the hazard space in absolute, range-scaled magnitude units. Each retained axis is scaled to the unit interval by its pool minimum and maximum, so that no single axis dominates distances while the spacing within each axis remains proportional to physical magnitude. *N* Latin hypercube anchors are drawn in the scaled hazard box, and each anchor is snapped to the nearest not-yet-selected pool member in Euclidean distance. The selector is deterministic given its anchor seed. It involves no iterative optimization of a coverage criterion and no tuning parameters.

Absolute-magnitude scaling is the operative choice, and it is what makes the design a genuine intervention rather than a variance-reduction tweak. Because the hazard marginals of a stochastic generator are strongly right-skewed, most pool members cluster near the center of the hazard space and few occupy the severe corners. Filling the range uniformly therefore draws selected members from the sparse corners far more often than their pool frequency, so the severe drought and flood conditions that decide reservoir performance are over-represented in the search ensemble relative to their probability under the generator. This is the deliberate distribution shift that RQ1 tests. An alternative that transforms each axis by its empirical cumulative distribution before filling would instead reproduce the pool's marginal frequencies and distort only the joint dependence among axes; that rank-space variant is registered as a non-campaign sensitivity, because it isolates how much of any effect is attributable to tail over-representation specifically, but it is not the campaign design.

The rationale for uniform coverage of a condition space, as opposed to probability-faithful sampling of it, descends from the bottom-up and decision-scaling traditions, which sample condition spaces uniformly to map system response rather than to estimate expectations (Brown et al., 2012; Culley et al., 2016; Herman et al., 2016; Fowler et al., 2024). We take from that tradition the coverage argument for constructing the ensemble, and not a precedent for biasing a reported robustness number. The selector is deliberately the simplest space-filling design so that its construction variance is measurable under replication (Section 5.2) and so that coverage statistics remain an independent verification of what the selector achieved, reported as a method diagnostic rather than as a comparison result (Supporting Information Section S5).

### 4.5 The consequence of the distribution shift

The two ensemble designs present different scenario distributions to the search. `fixed_probabilistic` presents the generator's own distribution, and `hazard_filling` presents a distribution deliberately shifted toward severe hazard conditions. Any objective aggregated over the search ensemble is therefore computed under a different measure for the two designs, and for `hazard_filling` that measure over-weights the conditions in which policies are hardest to satisfy. The shift biases which policies the optimizer selects, not merely a reported number, which is exactly why it is a design intervention worth testing and why search objectives are never compared across designs.

The held-out re-evaluation removes the evaluation bias of scoring each design on its own ensemble, because both designs are scored on the same instrument. It does not remove, and is not intended to remove, the selection bias that the hazard-filling construction embodies. That selection bias is the treatment under study. Whether over-representing severe hazard conditions during search yields policies that are more robust under held-out deeply uncertain re-evaluation, or whether it merely tailors policies to conditions that re-evaluation does not reward, is the empirical question, and the common test ensemble is what makes the two outcomes distinguishable. Importance-sampling reweighting of the search objectives back to the generator's measure is not attempted. The snapped-selection rule induces no tractable density, so exact weights are not available; and even an approximate reweighting would defeat the purpose of the intervention, which is to let severe conditions drive the search.

## 5. Experimental Design

### 5.1 Budget control and sizing

Both matched designs use *N* = 100 realizations of *L* = 10 years, which amounts to 1,000 scenario-years per function evaluation, and both run at equal NFE. Because *N* and *L* are common, the per-evaluation simulation cost, warm-up burden, scenario-years, and wall-clock time are identical across designs. Equal NFE and equal scenario-years therefore coincide, giving one budget condition and no confound between ensemble composition and search effort.

The common *N* and *L* are required rather than convenient. If *L* differed across designs, the selection rule would be confounded with record length, and at a fixed per-evaluation budget *N* and *L* trade off directly. The value of *N* is bounded below by the fill requirement. At a hazard space of four axes, *N* = 100 provides an average of 3.2 points per axis, and at three axes it provides 4.6, which we regard as the smallest defensible fill; the achieved coverage is reported against a random design of the same size and dimension so that this adequacy is demonstrated rather than assumed (Supporting Information Section S5). A long-record design, for example *L* = 50 years forcing *N* to approximately 20, would make space-filling in four dimensions meaningless. The value *L* = 10 years in turn exceeds the duration of the DRB drought of record in the 1960s, which lasted four to five years, plus its onset and recovery, so a design-basis event fits within a single window and duration-type hazard axes are not truncation-limited. The per-evaluation ensemble of 100 realizations is smaller than the 1,000-realization convention of the Zeff–Herman–Trindade lineage; the ground for it is the annual-unit pooling of Section 3.3, which yields 900 metric-bearing unit-years per evaluation, comparable to the sample on which that lineage stabilizes the same tail operators. The sampling variance of the worst-first-percentile operator at *N* = 100 is quantified by the objective-sensitivity experiment (Supporting Information Section S6). The `historic` design, with one realization of about 77 years, cannot be budget-matched and is reported as a reference throughout.

The function-evaluation budget targets 500,000 NFE per search, which is within the range used for comparable reservoir control-policy problems in this lineage (Quinn et al., 2017; Bartholomew and Kwakkel, 2020). [Open item: the campaign NFE is a target and may be revised once initial searches reveal the convergence behavior of the DRB problem at the campaign ensemble size; the runtime archive (Section 5.3) records intermediate NFE levels so that the attained budget can be justified after the fact and a lower budget adopted if search has plateaued.]

### 5.2 Replication: ensemble draws and search seeds

Two sources of variability are separated by design. A draw is a scenario design's entire construction re-run from scratch with a fresh seed. For `fixed_probabilistic` a draw is a fresh i.i.d. sample, and for `hazard_filling` a draw is a fresh candidate pool together with a fresh anchor plan. Re-drawing the pool within each draw is essential to the comparison. Generating the pool is part of the hazard-filling construction, and pinning it across draws would make hazard filling appear more stable than `fixed_probabilistic` as an artifact of the replication scheme rather than as a finding. A seed is an independent MOEA trial on a fixed draw. Each matched design runs *K* = 3 draws with *S* = 2 seeds per draw. [Open item: *K* and *S* are targets; the seed-versus-draw variance ratio is estimated from the first draws and the counts revised if seed variance is not small relative to draw variance.] The `historic` design has *K* = 1, because its composition variance is zero by construction, and is replicated across *S* seeds only.

The unit of analysis for the between-design comparison is the draw, and seeds within a draw are treated as pseudoreplicates. The comparison uses a mixed-effects structure in which the design is a fixed effect, the draw is a random effect nested within design, and the seed is a random effect nested within draw, so the effective sample size per design is approximately *K* rather than *K·S*. This replication structure separates ensemble-construction variance from search stochasticity. It is the feature that distinguishes the present comparison from the antecedent that most resembles it, Eker and Kwakkel (2018), which compared a single designed scenario set against a single random set by counting solutions and could not separate a genuine design effect from the variance of a single draw. [Open item: a pilot-based minimum-detectable-effect calculation on the primary endpoint (Section 6.3) is reported before *K* is fixed, so that a null result can be interpreted as evidence of a small effect rather than as insufficient power.]

### 5.3 Convergence reporting

Within each design, search progress is monitored through the algorithm's runtime dynamics and reported per seed as a diagnostic, following the runtime-metrics protocol of Reed et al. (2013) and the ε-progress and hypervolume conventions of Hadka and Reed (2015). These internal quantities are never compared across designs, because objective values computed on different search ensembles are not commensurable, and because reference-set metrics scored against a pooled frontier are biased in a design-dependent way (Section 6.5). Convergence assessment is not used as a stopping rule, so no design benefits from a tuned termination criterion, and both matched designs execute the identical NFE budget. Because the runtime archive records the approximation set at intermediate NFE levels, the final design comparison (Section 6) can be recomputed at two or three earlier budgets at re-evaluation cost only, which tests whether the design ranking is an artifact of the chosen budget rather than a stable result.

## 6. Held-Out Re-Evaluation and Comparison Metrics

### 6.1 The test ensemble E_test

Every design's final Pareto-approximate set is re-simulated, with the full untrimmed model, on a single common test ensemble E_test, and all cross-design comparison is made there. E_test is never used during any search, is never the source of any search ensemble, and is generated from a reserved seed domain disjoint from that of the candidate pool. A collision between the search-side and test-side seed domains is treated as a hard error, which guards against the failure mode of testing on data the search has seen (Bonham et al., 2024).

E_test is the largest ensemble in the study by a wide margin, and it is built to encompass a deeply uncertain climate-forcing envelope rather than to represent a probability distribution. The forcing is a monthly change factor applied to the generator's fitted moments, parameterized as a low-order harmonic in the log change factor whose amplitudes govern annual volume, seasonal amplitude, and the snowmelt-shoulder shape, with bounds taken from a CMIP6 multimodel ensemble (Supporting Information Section S2). E_test is a Latin hypercube over the full range of these forcing amplitudes, with an envelope deliberately wider than any variation the search ensembles contain, crossed with many stochastic realizations per Latin hypercube point. Each Latin hypercube point is a state of the world, and its *R*_test realizations sample natural variability within that state. Test records satisfy *L*_test ≥ *L* and are long enough to contain complete multi-year droughts. [Open item: *N*_θ,test, *R*_test, *L*_test, and the envelope width are fixed against the compute allocation; because the full model is only modestly more expensive than the trimmed model (Supporting Information Section S8), re-evaluation is a small fraction of the campaign cost, so E_test can be sized generously.] This construction, a Latin hypercube of states of the world crossed with stochastic realizations, is the standard of the re-evaluation lineage (Kasprzyk et al., 2013; Trindade et al., 2017; Bartholomew and Kwakkel, 2020; Gold et al., 2022).

Two properties of E_test carry the interpretation of the comparison. First, the search ensembles are drawn from the unperturbed stationary generator, whereas E_test spans a forced climate envelope that no search ensemble contains. The re-evaluation therefore measures whether hazard-space coverage of the natural-variability manifold produces policies that generalize to conditions absent from search, which is a stronger test than re-evaluating on the search distribution and which keeps the test instrument structurally distinct from both search designs. Second, no robustness number reported in this study is an expectation. Under deep uncertainty there is no probability measure over the forcing space, which is what the term deep denotes (Lamontagne et al., 2018). A satisficing fraction computed over E_test is a coverage-weighted count over a designed exploration of the deeply uncertain box, and it is reported as such rather than as an estimate of an expected value. The cross-design comparison is commensurable because E_test is identical across designs, not because it is probability-faithful.

Following Quinn et al. (2020), we do not claim that E_test is scenario-neutral, because no experimental design is, and all design rankings are conditional on this one reference ensemble. With a single construction, that conditioning is declared rather than bounded. A second, structurally different E_test construction, which replaces the Kirsch–Nowak structure with a hidden-Markov-model annual generator and thereby varies the generator family itself, is registered as a sensitivity that would allow the ranking's stability across test-ensemble constructions to be measured rather than assumed. [Open item: whether the second construction is stood up is a scope decision against the allocation; the persisted re-evaluation matrix (Section 6.2) additionally supports zero-simulation-cost composition-sensitivity checks, in which hazard-restricted and envelope-restricted subsets of the single E_test are re-scored to probe whether the ranking depends on the region of the test space emphasized.] We note that the default construction inherits the historical record's interannual wet-dry persistence, so persistence is stressed in neither the search population nor the test ensemble; Section 8 scopes the claims accordingly.

### 6.2 The persisted re-evaluation matrix

Re-evaluation persists the complete array of objective values, indexed by solution, realization, and objective, in natural units, together with each realization's state-of-the-world identifier. Every robustness metric in Section 6.3 is then an offline re-scoring of this array, and no metric choice, threshold change, or new metric requires re-simulation. In the taxonomy of McPhail et al. (2018), the persisted array is the substrate on which any performance transformation, scenario subset, and aggregation can be recomposed after the fact. Both natural units of robustness are thereby available. The realization unit applies the criterion across all *N*_θ,test × *R*_test realizations. The state-of-the-world unit of the regional-supply lineage collapses each forcing point's realizations first, by a stated within-state aggregator, and then applies the criterion across the *N*_θ,test states (Herman et al., 2014; Trindade et al., 2017; Gold et al., 2022, 2023). Precision is governed by *N*_θ,test rather than by the raw realization count, because realizations within a state are not independent, so all standard errors on ensemble-level quantities are clustered by forcing point, with a hierarchical bootstrap when the number of forcing points is small.

### 6.3 Comparison metrics

#### 6.3.1 The primary endpoint

The comparison endpoint for RQ1 is the re-evaluated multivariate satisficing fraction of the policies a design produces, under Starr's domain criterion. For a single policy, the satisficing fraction on E_test is

$$
S(x) \;=\; \frac{1}{N_{\text{test}}} \sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{test}}} \Lambda_j(x),
\qquad
\Lambda_j(x) \;=\; \prod_{i} \mathbf{1}\!\left[\, f_{i}(x, s_j) \;\text{meets}\; c_i \,\right],
$$

where $f_i(x, s_j)$ is policy *x*'s whole-trace temporal metric (Table 2) for objective *i* on test realization $s_j$, and $c_i$ is that objective's criterion. This is Starr's (1962) domain criterion, the standard robustness measure of the regional water-supply lineage (Herman et al., 2014, 2015; Trindade et al., 2017; Gold et al., 2022, 2023), and the fastest-converging robustness family in ensemble-size testing, with accurate rankings from 50 to 300 scenarios (Bonham et al., 2024).

A single search yields a Pareto-approximate set rather than one policy, so a run-level scalar is required before the mixed-effects comparison of Section 5.2 can operate. [Open item: the primary run-level endpoint is pre-specified before the campaign as the maximum satisficing fraction attained by any policy in the run's re-evaluated set. This choice answers the practical question of whether a design can find a robust policy at all, but it is a maximum over a set and therefore inherits a mild upward bias that grows with set cardinality; the leave-one-out reference-set correction of Section 6.5 and the co-reported robustness-space hypervolume of the run's set bound that bias, and the per-objective satisficing decomposition is reported alongside so that a single scalar does not carry the entire comparison. The alternative endpoints considered are the robustness-space hypervolume of the run's set and the satisficing fraction of a compromise policy chosen by a rule applied to search-side information only; the choice among them is fixed before the campaign and not after seeing E_test scores.]

The criteria $c_i$ are anchored on Decree and FFMP goalposts wherever one exists and are otherwise conventions, which is why they are swept (Section 6.4) rather than fixed at a single point. [Open item: the criterion values and the sweep grid.]

#### 6.3.2 Secondary metrics

Four secondary metrics are reported alongside the primary endpoint, all as offline re-scorings of the persisted matrix. Univariate satisficing gives the per-objective decomposition of the primary. The coverage-weighted mean across realizations is the risk-neutral reference, and the worst realization is the risk-averse reference; both are reported because metric choice is known to change robustness rankings (Herman et al., 2015; Giuliani and Castelletti, 2016; McPhail et al., 2018). The signed improvement over the status quo is the orientation-signed difference between each policy and the default 2017 FFMP policy simulated on the same E_test, which answers RQ2 by measuring whether re-optimization improves on current operations, and which serves as a fixed, design-independent reference in the deviation-from-baseline tradition of Kasprzyk et al. (2013). This last metric is a fixed-reference regret in the transformation taxonomy of McPhail et al. (2018); it is design-independent and does not move when designs are added or dropped, which is the property that a set-relative regret lacks (Section 6.3.3). Agreement among the design rankings induced by these metrics is summarized with Kendall's rank correlation τ_b (McPhail et al., 2020), and because a robustness scalar can be stable, optimizable, and still misleading (Huang et al., 2025), the raw performance distribution on E_test, summarized by its median and spread, is co-reported alongside every robustness value.

#### 6.3.3 Metrics deliberately excluded

No set-relative regret measure is computed. Best-in-set regret defines the reference by the best-performing policy in each scenario, so removing one design from the pooled set changes every other design's score, which violates the minimum requirement that a cross-design statistic be design-independent. It is also the slowest-converging metric family in Bonham et al. (2024), requiring more than 400 scenarios under their accuracy criterion and failing to converge within their tested range on a maximum-over-time objective, and our two deficit objectives are worst-first-percentile operators of exactly that type. Baseline regret in the sense of Cohen et al. (2021) requires one perfect-foresight optimization per scenario, which amounted to 3,233 CPU-hours in total across their 97 traces, is formulation-specific, and does not scale to candidate pools of 100,000 to 1,000,000 realized sequences; we cite it as motivation and never compute it. No search-minus-test overfitting gap is reported: Brodeur et al. (2020) diagnose overfitting graphically and define no gap metric, and under the hazard-filling design the in-sample term is computed under a deliberately shifted distribution, so the difference between the in-sample and held-out terms is an artifact of the distribution change that would grow with exactly the coverage the method advocates.

#### 6.3.4 Attainability screen

From the persisted array we flag, at no additional simulation cost, the E_test realizations in which no policy from either design meets the criteria. This screen separates poor search performance from scenarios that are unwinnable for the evaluated policy pool. Shavazipour et al. (2021) found that 23 percent of their test scenarios were unwinnable by any policy. Each design's satisficing fraction is bounded above by the attainable fraction, which sets the ceiling against which the design difference is read. The screen provides an empirical bound over the evaluated policies rather than a per-scenario oracle, since an oracle would require exactly the perfect-foresight optimization that this study excludes.

### 6.4 The criterion sweep

No study in this lineage derives its satisficing criteria. Where a criterion is not fixed by a Decree or FFMP goalpost, it descends from stakeholder elicitations (Zeff et al., 2014) and is a convention. We therefore anchor each criterion on a goalpost where one exists and sweep the remainder over a grid, asking not whether robustness values move, which they will, but whether the design difference is invariant. The sweep is necessary rather than cosmetic. Quinn et al. (2020) show that robustness-rank agreement across experimental designs degrades as the satisficing criterion becomes more stringent, so a single fixed threshold could manufacture or hide the entire design effect. Threshold-margin diagnostics, consisting of each objective's cumulative distribution across E_test with the criterion overlaid, follow Gold et al. (2023) and accompany the sweep. This is the sense in which the comparison is reported as a difference that holds, or fails to hold, across the range of defensible criteria, rather than as a single number at one arbitrary threshold.

### 6.5 Reference sets

Where dominance-based summaries over the pooled re-evaluated policies are reported, we report them against both the pooled reference set and a design-leave-one-out reference set, with the gap between the two measuring each design's self-reference inflation. A design contributes points to the frontier against which it is scored, the two designs return different numbers of solutions (an asymmetry that Bartholomew and Kwakkel (2020) acknowledge but do not correct), and noisier re-evaluated estimates contribute more spuriously nondominated points (Shavazipour et al., 2021). Within-design MOEA search metrics, including hypervolume, are computed against per-design reference sets only and read purely as convergence diagnostics, following the protocol of Zatarain Salazar et al. (2017), who established that reference sets must be built per search-ensemble level and that cross-level comparison is defensible only on a common independent verification ensemble. These summaries are supplementary; the primary comparison is the endpoint of Section 6.3 on E_test.

## 7. Computational Experiment

All searches use the Multi-Master Borg MOEA (Hadka and Reed, 2013, 2015), whose epsilon-dominance archiving, adaptive operator selection, and randomized restarts have repeatedly proven reliable for many-objective water resources problems (Reed et al., 2013; Zatarain Salazar et al., 2016, 2017). The per-evaluation cost of the campaign ensemble configuration was measured directly on the Purdue Anvil system rather than extrapolated: at 128 evaluator ranks per node, one function evaluation over the campaign ensemble (100 realizations of 10 years on the trimmed model) takes a median of 173.8 s, and the full model used at re-evaluation is only 1.16 times as expensive (Supporting Information Section S8). Combined with the measured strong-scaling efficiency, one 500,000-NFE search costs approximately 33,000 service units, and the matched campaign of two designs at three draws and two seeds each, together with the cheap `historic` reference and the re-evaluation phase, is a minority of the available allocation, leaving reserve for the variable-resolution sweep, the additional draws that a power calculation may indicate, and the optional second test-ensemble construction. Each combination of design, draw, and seed is an independent job, and every function evaluation simulates the trimmed model over the design's 100-realization ensemble. Runtime archive snapshots are logged at a fixed NFE interval for the convergence diagnostics of Section 5.3, and the epsilon precisions are the per-objective values of Table 2 for the historic design, with the ensemble designs' annual-unit epsilons set by the objective-sensitivity experiment. ## 8. Anticipated Threats to Validity

Four threats structure how the results will be read, and we state them before any result exists.

The first threat is the possibility that designed selection provides no benefit. Eker and Kwakkel (2018) found that diversity-based scenario selection did not outperform randomly selected scenarios on the lake problem. The contrast between `fixed_probabilistic` and `hazard_filling` is that benchmark with greater statistical power and on a real system: our selection is in hazard space rather than in outcome space on a stylized benchmark, and the comparison rests on independent ensemble draws with the draw as the unit of analysis rather than on solution counting. If hazard filling does not outperform random sampling under this design, the result is a well-powered null, and we will report it as such. The reframing of this study around a single controlled contrast, rather than a larger set of designs, is deliberate: it makes that null, if it occurs, interpretable.

The second threat is the search-measure mismatch penalty. Giuliani and Castelletti (2016) show that policies designed under one across-scenario measure and evaluated under another are systematically dominated by policies designed under the evaluation measure. Hazard filling searches under a distribution deliberately shifted toward severe conditions and is scored under E_test's composition, so the mismatch penalty is the expected direction of the effect under the null hypothesis, and any hazard-filling advantage must overcome it. We state this as the study's null hypothesis rather than leaving it to be identified in review.

The third threat is selection bias, which this study measures rather than corrects. Bartholomew and Kwakkel (2020) articulate the objection that applies to any designed scenario selection, writing that it "intrinsically biases subsequent results towards solutions that do well in this region," with no a priori guarantee against vulnerability elsewhere. We do not dispute this. The distribution shift is deliberate, stated, and coverage-motivated; the held-out re-evaluation corrects evaluation bias only; and the residual selection effect, including any induced vulnerability in under-represented regions, is precisely the quantity the experiment measures on the common test ensemble.

The fourth threat concerns conditioning and scope. All rankings are conditional on the single E_test construction (Section 6.1); robustness values are coverage-weighted counts rather than expectations; and the forcing space perturbs monthly means and variability but not interannual persistence, so every claim is scoped to hold under historical persistence, in this basin, for this rule structure and objective set. The two ensemble designs also differ in the variance of their fitness estimates, since a frozen i.i.d. ensemble and a frozen coverage-designed ensemble present different sampling structures to the optimizer; this is matched in budget but not in kind, and is disclosed rather than removed. The design most exposed to a favorable-instrument objection under the mismatch-penalty logic would be one whose search distribution resembled E_test, and neither matched design is constructed that way: both search on the unperturbed generator while E_test spans a forced envelope, so E_test does not structurally favor either design over the other.
