# Scenario reduction & generation (stochastic programming and energy analogues)

*Paper 3 literature note. Annotated summaries of items in the Zotero collection "Paper 3 NYC Reoptimization" (`ISYGLK35`). Last updated 2026-06-16.*

Scope: the classical stochastic-programming pedigree for constructing and reducing scenario sets, plus the energy-sector feature-space analogues. Every branch here defines a good subset as **representative in probability** — the precise foil for a uniform-in-hazard-space subsample (see the framing distinctions in the [scenario design overview](scenario_design.md)). Retained in trimmed form because the audience is MOEA-based: each entry anchors the foil, supplies the i.i.d. baseline, shows scenario-design comparison is publishable, or admits problem-driven concentration on hazardous scenarios.

---

- **Dupačová, J., Gröwe-Kuska, N. & Römisch, W. (2003).** Scenario reduction in stochastic programming. *Mathematical Programming*, 95(3), 493-511. doi:10.1007/s10107-002-0331-0
  The founding scenario-reduction paper. Given a discrete distribution it selects a subset of prescribed cardinality plus a redistributed probability measure that minimizes a **Fortet–Mourier probability metric** (a Kantorovich/Wasserstein-type mass-transportation distance), justified by **quantitative stability** of the optimal value under measure perturbation. The Kantorovich functional for a fixed retained subset and its **optimal redistribution rule** (deleted mass goes to the nearest kept scenario) are derived in closed form, so no transportation LP is solved per evaluation. Two heuristics for the combinatorial subset choice are given: **backward reduction** (recursively delete) and **forward selection** (recursively add); forward gives slightly better trees at higher CPU cost. Demonstrated on electrical-load scenario trees: deleting 50% of scenarios loses only ~10% relative accuracy. The canonical representative-in-probability approach the proposed method departs from.

- **Rujeerapaiboon, N., Schindler, K., Kuhn, D. & Wiesemann, W. (2022).** Scenario reduction revisited: fundamental limits and guarantees. *Mathematical Programming*, 191(1), 207-242. doi:10.1007/s10107-018-1269-1
  Revisits Dupačová-style reduction under the **type-l Wasserstein distance**, distinguishing **continuous** reduction (new atoms placed freely) from **discrete** reduction (atoms drawn from the original support), and bounds the added benefit of the former. Identifies the worst-case n-point distributions least amenable to reduction (maximum Wasserstein distance to any m-point approximation) and links the problem to **k-means / k-median clustering**. Contributes the first **polynomial-time constant-factor approximation algorithms** for both variants plus exact exponential-time algorithms for the continuous case. Recent, top-venue evidence that the field still defines optimal subsets by distributional proximity, with explicit complexity guarantees rather than heuristics.

- **Homem-de-Mello, T. & Bayraksan, G. (2014).** Monte Carlo sampling-based methods for stochastic optimization. *Surveys in Operations Research and Management Science*, 19(1), 56-85. doi:10.1016/j.sorms.2014.05.001
  Authoritative survey of **sample average approximation (SAA)** (a.k.a. sample-path / stochastic-counterpart optimization): convergence of optimal values and solutions, optimality-gap estimation and stopping rules, and sample-size selection balancing optimization against estimation error. Reviews the **variance-reduction** toolkit — importance sampling, **Latin Hypercube sampling**, quasi-Monte Carlo, control variates — and multiple/single-replication gap procedures (MRP, SRP, 2RP/A2RP). The i.i.d. probabilistic baseline; documents that LHS on input variables is accepted SAA practice, against which hazard-space stratification is the novelty.

- **Kaut, M. & Wallace, S. W. (2007).** Evaluation of scenario generation methods for stochastic programming. *Pacific Journal of Optimization*, 3(2), 257-271.
  Defines the **minimal quality requirements** for any scenario set used in optimization, framed around the (usually uncomputable) **optimality gap** between true and scenario-based solutions. **In-sample stability** (objective values agree across independently generated trees of the same input) is always checkable; **out-of-sample stability** (true objective of each tree's solution agrees, ideally evaluated on an exogenous reference/benchmark tree) detects bias and requires evaluating the true objective. The two are shown to be logically independent (a mean/variance example dissociates them). Argues quality is problem-specific — judge by decision quality, not distributional fidelity. These map directly onto the experimental design (hypervolume stability across replicate subsamples and seeds; re-evaluation bias on the full ensemble).

- **Löhndorf, N. (2016).** An empirical analysis of scenario generation methods for stochastic optimization. *European Journal of Operational Research*, 255(1), 121-132. doi:10.1016/j.ejor.2016.05.021
  Empirically benchmarks scenario-construction methods — Monte Carlo, quasi-Monte Carlo, **moment matching**, probability-metric **optimal quantization**, and a proposed **Voronoi cell sampling** — by **objective error** and **policy error** on an analytically solvable multi-dimensional newsvendor problem, including a **CVaR / expected-shortfall** (risk-averse) variant solved via Benders decomposition. No method dominates: optimal quantization wins in low dimensions, moment matching for normal/uniform, QMC for log-normal; Voronoi cell sampling gives the lowest errors overall and excels on heavy-tailed distributions. Controversially reports probability-metric methods are **ineffective under high-dimensional randomness**. The precedent that scenario-design comparison is publishable methodology; the planned campaign is its many-objective, simulation-based analogue with hazard-space designs added.

- **Fairbrother, J., Turner, A. & Wallace, S. W. (2022).** Problem-driven scenario generation: an analytical approach for stochastic programs with tail risk measure. *Mathematical Programming*, 191(1), 141-182. doi:10.1007/s10107-019-01451-7
  Formalizes **problem-driven** scenario generation for objectives using a **tail risk measure** (e.g., CVaR): the measure depends only on scenarios inside a problem-specific **risk region**, so all scenarios outside it can be aggregated into a single point. The proposed **aggregation sampling** algorithm draws until a target count of risk-region samples is reached and collapses the rest, with proofs of **asymptotic consistency** with standard Monte Carlo. Risk regions are derived as proof-of-concept for monotonic-loss (network design) and portfolio-selection problems; numerical tests show better and more stable solutions than plain Monte Carlo. The classical school's own admission that the right set depends on what drives the objectives — the logic behind concentrating evaluation on hazardous conditions.

- **Chou, X. & Messina, E. (2023).** Problem-driven scenario generation for stochastic programming problems: a survey. *Algorithms*, 16(10), 479. doi:10.3390/a16100479
  Surveys and categorizes **problem-driven** scenario-generation methods, contrasting them with distribution-driven approaches and emphasizing the tension between including informative scenarios and **overfitting** to the sample. Identifies when problem structure can be exploited for a more concise representation and discusses integration with **machine-learning** techniques. Situates hazard-/outcome-aware scenario design within the broader stochastic-programming literature.

- **Hilbers, A. P., Brayshaw, D. J. & Gandy, A. (2019).** Importance subsampling: improving power system planning under climate-based uncertainty. *Applied Energy*, 251, 113114. doi:10.1016/j.apenergy.2019.04.110
  Introduces **importance subsampling**: from decades of hourly demand/weather data, deliberately over-represent rare high-cost periods and **reweight** the retained sample so the reduced set remains an unbiased estimator of system cost. Outperforms using single years or **representative-day clustering**, which underestimate cost and produce capacity-adequacy shortfalls; the subsampled designs meet demand across all 36 years. The closest energy-sector analogue for biasing an optimization ensemble toward the hazardous tail; the proposed design generalizes this to multi-dimensional hazard space without reweighting. (summary from abstract/metadata; fulltext unavailable)

- **Nahmmacher, P., Schmid, E., Hirth, L. & Knopf, B. (2016).** Carpe diem: a novel approach to select representative days for long-term power system modeling. *Energy*, 112, 430-442. doi:10.1016/j.energy.2016.06.081
  Selects weighted **representative days** for long-term power-system models by clustering full-dimensional **feature vectors** of demand, wind and solar time series, extending earlier load-only time-slice selection to inputs with many fluctuating series. Validated on the LIMES-EU model: few model days reproduce the characteristic fluctuations of the input data. Demonstrates feature-space subset selection is mainstream in energy planning, but chosen to reproduce the joint distribution rather than cover hazard space uniformly.

- **Teichgraeber, H. & Brandt, A. R. (2022).** Time-series aggregation for the optimization of energy systems: goals, challenges, approaches, and opportunities. *Renewable and Sustainable Energy Reviews*, 157, 111984. doi:10.1016/j.rser.2021.111984
  Authoritative recent review of **time-series aggregation** for energy-systems optimization: where time series enter optimization models, the assumptions and challenges of reduction, and the method families (representative periods, clustering). Recommends best practices — measure performance by **optimization outcome validated on the full series**, tune aggregation and formulation jointly to the problem, treat wind data carefully, **bound the objective-function error**, and **explicitly include real extreme days**. Transferable guidance for designing and validating reduced streamflow ensembles; its designs remain distribution-representative.
