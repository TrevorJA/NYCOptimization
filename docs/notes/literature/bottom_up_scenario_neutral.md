# Bottom-up & scenario-neutral design

*Paper 3 literature note. Annotated summaries of items in the Zotero collection "Paper 3 NYC Reoptimization" (`ISYGLK35`). Last updated 2026-06-16.*

Scope: the bottom-up / scenario-neutral / decision-scaling tradition that samples a **condition (exposure) space** uniformly and stress-tests system performance over it, independent of projection likelihoods. This is the intellectual ancestor of the proposed approach and the sharpest contrast: these studies sample climate-attribute *exposure* space (often via generator inversion), whereas the proposed method subsamples *realized streamflow* in hazard space. Directly informs the deeply-uncertain test-ensemble design. Generators themselves are in [stochastic streamflow generation](stochastic_streamflow_generation.md).

---

- **Brown, C., Ghile, Y., Laverty, M. & Li, K. (2012).** Decision scaling: Linking bottom-up vulnerability analysis with climate projections in the water sector. *Water Resources Research*, 48(9), W09537. doi:10.1029/2011WR011212
  Founding statement of decision scaling, which *inverts* the top-down GCM-driven impact chain: a decision-system model is run first, then climate is treated as a state variable. A three-step process (identify thresholds; stochastic risk discovery; tailor climate information) parses a climate space (mean precipitation, temperature departures) into decision states. A **climate response function** g(v_T) is fitted as a surrogate linking aggregate climate statistics to a performance indicator, enabling cheap evaluation of large GCM ensembles. GCM projections are used only as a *posterior weighting* on stochastically discovered risks, not as scenario generators. Demonstrated on a stylized Quabbin-Wachusett urban supply system using 50,000 years of stochastic net-basin-supply and parametric gridded sampling of mean climate. Source for the move to system-relevant condition dimensions over projection-driven design.

- **Prudhomme, C., Wilby, R. L., Crooks, S., Kay, A. L. & Reynard, N. S. (2010).** Scenario-neutral approach to climate change impact studies: Application to flood risk. *Journal of Hydrology*, 390(3-4), 198-209. doi:10.1016/j.jhydrol.2010.06.043
  Coined "scenario-neutral" for an explicit **sensitivity response-surface** design that separates climate change hazard from catchment vulnerability. Monthly change factors (delta/perturbation method) are summarized by a single-phase harmonic, reducing each scenario to three parameters (mean annual change, seasonal amplitude, phase). A factorial grid (525 precipitation × 8 temperature = 4,200 combinations) is run through a calibrated PDM rainfall-runoff model to map RP20 flood-peak change as a response surface over a 2-D exposure space (mean change vs. seasonality), independent of projections. GCM/RCM projection clouds are then *overlaid* on the surface to compute the fraction exceeding the UK 20% design allowance. Two contrasting UK catchments. Establishes uniform gridded coverage of a condition space and reusability of one surface across new projection sets.

- **Guo, D., Westra, S. & Maier, H. R. (2018).** An inverse approach to perturb historical rainfall data for scenario-neutral climate impact studies. *Journal of Hydrology*, 556, 877-890. doi:10.1016/j.jhydrol.2016.03.025
  Develops an optimization-based **stochastic generator inversion** so sampled points fall uniformly across a multi-attribute rainfall **exposure space**, because forward (parameter) sampling of a generator covers the attribute space unevenly. Perturbs not only the mean but intermittency and extremes, using the WGEN generator: an optimizer searches WGEN parameters that reproduce each target attribute vector, yielding evenly distributed coverage. Adelaide, Australia case study. The closest scenario-neutral analogue to the project's input-vs-hazard redundancy argument, articulated for rainfall attributes rather than streamflow; the canonical citation for "uniform exposure-space coverage requires inverting the generator."

- **Culley, S., Noble, S., Yates, A., Timbs, M., Westra, S., Maier, H. R., Giuliani, M. & Castelletti, A. (2016).** A bottom-up approach to identifying the maximum operational adaptive capacity of water resource systems to a changing climate. *Water Resources Research*, 52(9), 6751-6768. doi:10.1002/2015WR018253
  Extends decision scaling by **re-optimizing the operating policy at every point of the exposure space**. Builds an n-dimensional exposure space H by incrementally perturbing hydrometeorological attributes (uniformly sampled between plausible bounds), then for each "hydrometeorological state" uses an evolutionary algorithm to optimize a feedback control policy minimizing failure criteria. Comparing the success subspace under historical vs. state-specific optimal policies defines "maximum operational adaptive capacity" (ratio of satisfactory states), a ceiling beyond which infrastructure upgrades become unavoidable. Lake Como, Italy (supply vs. flood control). Closest precedent for coupling bottom-up condition spaces with policy optimization, and an authoritative source for "exposure space" and per-state re-optimization.

- **Fowler, K. J. A., McMahon, T. A., Westra, S., Horne, A., Guillaume, J. H. A., Guo, D., Nathan, R., Maier, H. R. & John, A. (2024).** Climate stress testing for water systems: Review and guide for applications. *WIREs Water*, 11(6), e1747. doi:10.1002/wat2.1747
  Current consolidating review and practitioner guide for bottom-up **climate stress testing**. Organizes practice around stressor selection, characterizing and exploring the n-dimensional exposure space (one dimension per stressor; explicitly "analogous to the climate space of Brown et al. 2012"), and data generation including stochastic weather/streamflow generators. Catalogs analysis modes including delineating failure/non-failure domains and **scenario discovery** via factor mapping (PRIM, CART). Provides a vetted glossary (exposure space, stressor, failure domain) and frames stress testing as suited to deep uncertainty and stakeholder legitimacy. Primary terminology and framing citation for the bottom-up thread.

- **Quinn, J. D., Reed, P. M., Giuliani, M., Castelletti, A., Oyler, J. W. & Nicholas, R. E. (2018).** Exploring how changing monsoonal dynamics and human pressures challenge multireservoir management for flood protection, hydropower production, and agricultural water supply. *Water Resources Research*, 54(7), 4638-4662. doi:10.1029/2018WR022743
  MORDM analysis generating **states of the world (SOWs)** through a design of experiments over generator and demand parameters. Monthly flows are produced by Cholesky-decomposition (Kirsch) generation with Nowak nearest-neighbor daily disaggregation, then rescaled to perturb mean flow, within-year monsoon amplitude, and interannual variability; policies optimized assuming stationarity are re-evaluated across these SOWs and analyzed by **scenario discovery** (satisficing robustness plus sensitivity analysis). Red River, Vietnam (flood, hydropower, supply). The key contrast paper: sampling generator/input parameters does not control where realizations land in hazard space, and overlapping parameterizations can produce redundant hazard conditions, while flood risk is driven by mechanisms beyond mean change.

- **Quinn, J. D., Hadjimichael, A., Reed, P. M. & Steinschneider, S. (2020).** Can exploratory modeling of water scarcity vulnerabilities and robustness be scenario neutral? *Earth's Future*, 8(11), e2020EF001650. doi:10.1029/2020EF001650
  Directly tests whether scenario-neutral exploratory modeling is truly neutral by comparing four experimental designs over the parameters of an HMM streamflow generator: (1) scenario-neutral box around historical, (2) CMIP projection-informed, (3) paleo-informed, (4) an all-encompassing scenario-neutral box spanning the others. **Sobol variance-based sensitivity analysis** and fitted **response surfaces** show that assumptions of uniformity and independence among uncertain factors change which drivers appear most important and reorder user robustness rankings, with inconsistencies growing under more conservative performance criteria. Upper Colorado River Basin, multi-user prior-appropriation allocation. The anchor evidence that input-space framings give uneven, design-dependent hazard coverage and that the uniformity/independence assumptions are consequential, not innocuous.
