# Objective & robustness formulations

*Paper 3 literature note. Annotated summaries of items in the Zotero collection "Paper 3 NYC Reoptimization" (`ISYGLK35`). Last updated 2026-06-16.*

Scope: how the optimization problem is *posed* — objective/problem formulation choices and the definition, calculation, and selection of **robustness metrics** (including across-scenario aggregation). Supports the objective-formulation decision. The scenario-design notes reference this note wherever they invoke a robustness metric or aggregation rule. The end-to-end workflows that apply these formulations are in [DU optimization workflows](du_optimization_workflows.md).

---

### Objective / problem formulation

- **Kasprzyk, J. R., Nataraj, S., Reed, P. M. & Lempert, R. J. (2013).** Many objective robust decision making for complex environmental systems undergoing change. *Environmental Modelling & Software*, 42, 55-71. doi:10.1016/j.envsoft.2012.12.007
  Introduces Many-Objective Robust Decision Making (MORDM), the framework this study instantiates. Workflow: (1) problem formulation with XLRM/scenario structuring; (2) many-objective evolutionary search (ε-NSGA-II) to approximate the Pareto set under a baseline state of the world (SOW); (3) re-evaluation of each solution across an ensemble of deeply uncertain SOW to assess robustness; (4) scenario discovery (PRIM) to identify the factor ranges driving vulnerability. Robustness is operationalized as Pareto-satisficing behaviour and a percent-deviation metric (each objective's degradation versus its baseline value at the 90th/10th percentile across SOW), rather than aggregating objectives into a single utility. Demonstrated on Lower Rio Grande Valley water supply; including robustness as a decision criterion materially reshapes problem formulation and the selected alternatives.

- **Quinn, J. D., Reed, P. M., Giuliani, M. & Castelletti, A. (2017).** Rival framings: A framework for discovering how problem formulation uncertainties shape risk management trade-offs in water resources systems. *Water Resources Research*, 53(8), 7208-7233. doi:10.1002/2017WR020524
  Advances a "rival framings" approach treating the problem formulation itself as uncertain: four competing multi-objective formulations of the Red River (Vietnam) four-reservoir control problem are optimized, varying not only which objectives/constraints are included but the *mathematical quantification* of each objective across a gradient of risk attitudes — from risk-averse min-max/worst-case to risk-neutral expected-value framings. Solutions from each framing are cross-re-evaluated on every other framing's objectives to expose systematic biases and regrets (after Maass's four-step design and Savage regret). Key findings: minimizing *expected* flood damage perversely raises catastrophic-flood risk in favour of hydropower, and min-max objectives common in robust optimization give unstable, poorly representative trade-offs. Anchors the choice and explicit disclosure of objective formulations.

### Robustness metrics & definitions

- **McPhail, C., Maier, H. R., Kwakkel, J. H., Giuliani, M., Castelletti, A. & Westra, S. (2018).** Robustness metrics: How are they calculated, when should they be used and why do they give different results? *Earth's Future*, 6(2), 169-191. doi:10.1002/2017EF000649
  Provides the unifying decomposition this note adopts: any robustness metric is built from three choices — T1, the performance value type (absolute performance vs **regret**, set by decision context); T2, the scenario subset over which performance is aggregated (all SOW, worst-case, worst-half, percentile); and T3, the statistical-moment summary (expected value, higher-order moments such as mean-variance, percentile-based skewness/peakedness), encoding risk aversion. Catalogues maximin/Wald, maximax, Hurwicz optimism-pessimism, Laplace principle of insufficient reason, minimax regret, undesirable-deviations, and satisficing/domain-criterion metrics within this scheme, and supplies a conceptual framework predicting when metric-based *rankings* of alternatives agree or disagree (ranking stability). Tested on three water cases (Adelaide supply, an Italian multipurpose lake, a Rhine-reach flood problem). The implementation scaffold for selecting robustness metrics and across-scenario aggregation.

- **Giuliani, M. & Castelletti, A. (2016).** Is robustness really robust? How different definitions of robustness impact decision-making under climate change. *Climatic Change*, 135(3-4), 409-424. doi:10.1007/s10584-015-1586-9
  Optimizes Lake Como operating policies (EMODPS, Borg MOEA, RBF policies) across a 28-member climate-change SOW ensemble under five classical decision-theoretic robustness metrics spanning an optimism-pessimism spectrum: maximin (Wald), maximax, Hurwicz optimism-pessimism, minimax regret (Savage), and Laplace principle of insufficient reason. Each metric is the across-scenario aggregation operator in the search, yielding distinct Pareto sets and trade-off shapes. Misdefining the decision-maker's attitude biases simulated decisions and systematically underestimates performance (worse for more pessimistic metrics); rankings conflict sharply across metrics, and preferences can evolve dynamically over time. Argues the robustness *definition* should itself be treated as an uncertain factor and that multiple metrics be used.

- **Lempert, R. J. & Collins, M. T. (2007).** Managing the risk of uncertain threshold responses: Comparison of robust, optimum, and precautionary approaches. *Risk Analysis*, 27(4), 1009-1026. doi:10.1111/j.1539-6924.2007.00940.x
  Compares decision frameworks on a lake-eutrophication threshold model under deep uncertainty: optimal expected utility, the precautionary principle, and three robust-decision approaches — trading optimal performance for reduced sensitivity to assumptions, **satisficing** across a wide range of futures, and keeping options open (flexibility). Robustness is evaluated over a landscape of plausible probability distributions, with expected regret (Savage-style) computed across SOW. The three robust approaches converge on similar strategies, offer a quantitative analytic expression of the precautionary principle's intent, and are preferable to the expected-utility optimum when uncertainty is sufficiently deep and the option set sufficiently rich. Foundational comparison of robustness framings (satisficing vs regret vs flexibility vs optimality).

- **Huang, J., Sangiorgio, M., Wu, W., Maier, H. R., Wang, Q. J., Hughes, J. & Castelletti, A. (2025).** Solving the robustness puzzle: The joint impact of optimization approach, robustness metrics, and scenarios on water resources management under deep uncertainty. *Journal of Environmental Management*, 373, 123540. doi:10.1016/j.jenvman.2024.123540
  Systematically isolates the joint influence of three axes — the same axes this study manipulates — using McPhail et al.'s (2018) T1/T2/T3 framework. Axis 1: optimization approach, contrasting a-posteriori "Pareto-optimal-then-robustness-assessment" (PORA, optimize on a reference scenario then screen for robustness) against robust optimization (RO, embedding the robustness metric directly in the search). Axis 2: robustness metric (maximax/best-case, maximin/worst-case, Hurwicz, regret-, percentile-, and moment-based aggregations). Axis 3: the scenario set used during evaluation. Two contrasting case studies (upper Adelaide River dam water supply; Lake Como multipurpose planning). Finds RO yields comparable or improved robustness versus PORA, and that approach, metric, and scenario-set choices interact to change which solution is preferred — directly motivating treating these as coupled design decisions.
