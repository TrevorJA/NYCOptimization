# Objective & robustness formulations

*Paper 3 literature note. Annotated summaries of items in the Zotero collection "Paper 3 NYC Reoptimization" (`ISYGLK35`). Last updated 2026-07-13.*

Scope: how the optimization problem is *posed* — objective/problem formulation choices and the definition, calculation, and selection of **robustness metrics** (including across-scenario aggregation). Supports the objective-formulation decision and the held-out re-evaluation metric set (`methods/objective_definitions.md` §3). The scenario-design notes reference this note wherever they invoke a robustness metric or aggregation rule. The end-to-end workflows that apply these formulations are in [DU optimization workflows](du_optimization_workflows.md).

### Objective / problem formulation

- **Kasprzyk, J. R., Nataraj, S., Reed, P. M. & Lempert, R. J. (2013).** Many objective robust decision making for complex environmental systems undergoing change. *Environmental Modelling & Software*, 42, 55-71. doi:10.1016/j.envsoft.2012.12.007
  Introduces Many-Objective Robust Decision Making (MORDM), the framework this study instantiates. Workflow: (1) problem formulation with XLRM/scenario structuring; (2) many-objective evolutionary search (ε-NSGA-II) to approximate the Pareto set under a baseline state of the world (SOW); (3) re-evaluation of each solution across an ensemble of deeply uncertain SOW to assess robustness; (4) scenario discovery (PRIM) to identify the factor ranges driving vulnerability. Robustness is operationalized as Pareto-satisficing behaviour and a percent-deviation metric (each objective's degradation versus its baseline value at the 90th/10th percentile across SOW), rather than aggregating objectives into a single utility. Demonstrated on Lower Rio Grande Valley water supply; including robustness as a decision criterion materially reshapes problem formulation and the selected alternatives. The percent-deviation-from-baseline form is the precedent for the *improvement-over-status-quo* secondary metric (deviation from the default FFMP policy on the same test ensemble).

- **Quinn, J. D., Reed, P. M., Giuliani, M. & Castelletti, A. (2017).** Rival framings: A framework for discovering how problem formulation uncertainties shape risk management trade-offs in water resources systems. *Water Resources Research*, 53(8), 7208-7233. doi:10.1002/2017WR020524
  Advances a "rival framings" approach treating the problem formulation itself as uncertain: four competing multi-objective formulations of the Red River (Vietnam) four-reservoir control problem are optimized, varying not only which objectives/constraints are included but the *mathematical quantification* of each objective across a gradient of risk attitudes — from risk-averse min-max/worst-case to risk-neutral expected-value framings. Solutions from each framing are cross-re-evaluated on every other framing's objectives to expose systematic biases and regrets (after Maass's four-step design and Savage regret). Key findings: minimizing *expected* flood damage perversely raises catastrophic-flood risk in favour of hydropower, and min-max objectives common in robust optimization give unstable, poorly representative trade-offs. Anchors the choice and explicit disclosure of objective formulations.

### Robustness metric taxonomy

- **McPhail, C., Maier, H. R., Kwakkel, J. H., Giuliani, M., Castelletti, A. & Westra, S. (2018).** Robustness metrics: How are they calculated, when should they be used and why do they give different results? *Earth's Future*, 6(2), 169-191. doi:10.1002/2017EF000649
  The unifying decomposition this project adopts. Any robustness metric is three choices: **T1** the performance-value transform — identity (absolute performance), **regret**, or **satisfaction-of-constraints** (threshold/satisficing); **T2** the scenario subset aggregated over — all scenarios, worst case, worst half, a percentile; **T3** the aggregation function over that subset — mean/expected value, variance and higher moments, worst case, percentile-based skewness or peakedness. Eleven common metrics are placed in the scheme: maximin (Wald), maximax, Hurwicz optimism-pessimism, Laplace's principle of insufficient reason (= mean), minimax regret (Savage), 90th-percentile minimax regret, mean-variance, undesirable deviations, percentile-based skewness, percentile-based peakedness, and **Starr's domain criterion** (satisficing). Metrics disagree systematically because they differ in T1 and T3: regret-type (T1) metrics reward *relative* improvement and so re-rank alternatives whenever the comparison set changes; worst-case (T3) metrics are driven by the tail and are unstable under sampling noise; satisficing (T1 = constraint satisfaction, T3 = frequency) is bounded and stable. Tested on three water cases (Adelaide supply, an Italian multipurpose lake, a Rhine flood reach). This is the scaffold used to classify every aggregation in `objective_definitions.md`.

- **McPhail, C., Maier, H. R., Westra, S., Kwakkel, J. H. & van der Linden, L. (2020).** Impact of scenario selection on robustness. *Water Resources Research*, 56(9), e2019WR026515. doi:10.1029/2019WR026515
  Values versus rankings. Scenario-set composition has a large effect on absolute robustness *values* but a comparatively small effect on the *ranking* of alternatives: Kendall's τ_b between rankings under different scenario sets is mostly 0.75-1.0. The exceptions matter and are the metrics to avoid as a primary: **percentile-based skewness and peakedness**, **undesirable deviations**, and **maximax under a reliability performance metric**, whose rankings are not stable under a change of scenario set. Consequences adopted here: report rankings (with τ_b), not values, when the ensemble composition is what is being manipulated; hold the re-evaluation metric fixed across designs (the moving-measuring-stick guard); and keep the primary metric out of the unstable class. Full subsampling-methodology annotation in [scenario subset selection](scenario_subset_selection.md).

- **Bonham, N., Kasprzyk, J., Zagona, E. & Rajagopalan, B. (2024).** Subsampling and space-filling metrics to test ensemble size for robustness analysis with a demonstration in the Colorado River Basin. *Environmental Modelling & Software*, 172, 105933. doi:10.1016/j.envsoft.2023.105933
  Supplies the **convergence bands** that decide which metrics are computable at a given ensemble size. Robustness is recomputed on subsamples of a 500-SOW Colorado River database and ranked against the full-set ranking with Kendall's τ_b. **Satisficing metrics converge fastest — accurate rankings from ~50-300 scenarios** (50 vulnerability, 250 reliability, 300 resiliency). **Regret-from-best needs 400+ scenarios**, and on a **max-over-time** objective it **never converges** within the available ensemble; the paper cautions explicitly against using regret-from-best in isolation. Also notes the degeneracy failure mode: a satisficing metric can *saturate*, so every alternative ties and the ranking carries no information. Together these findings are why satisficing is the primary re-evaluation metric here and why regret-from-best is excluded (our two deficit objectives are worst-1st-percentile operators — exactly the extreme-of-extremes case that fails to converge). Full annotation in [scenario subset selection](scenario_subset_selection.md).

### Satisficing, the domain criterion, and the threshold problem

- **Herman, J. D., Reed, P. M., Zeff, H. B. & Characklis, G. W. (2015).** How should robustness be defined for water systems planning under change? *Journal of Water Resources Planning and Management*, 141(10), 04015012. doi:10.1061/(ASCE)WR.1943-5452.0000509
  The anchor for the primary metric. Decomposes any robustness framing into four choices — how alternatives are generated, how the SOW ensemble is sampled, how performance is transformed (T1), and how it is aggregated across SOW (T3) — and compares expected-value, regret-based, and satisficing framings on the Research Triangle water-supply portfolio problem. Recommends the **multivariate satisficing (Starr's domain criterion)** form for multi-stakeholder water planning: the fraction of SOW in which an alternative meets *all* performance criteria jointly, with the **SOW as the unit of analysis**. Key findings: robustness definitions that aggregate performance into a single expected value or that measure regret against a shifting comparison set can reverse the preference ordering; the choice of ensemble sampling interacts with the choice of metric; and multivariate satisficing best exposes conflicts between stakeholders. Multivariate satisficing over the held-out test ensemble is the primary comparison metric in `objective_definitions.md` §3.1, with univariate satisficing as its per-objective decomposition.

- **Starr, M. K. (1962).** *Product Design and Decision Theory.* Prentice-Hall, Englewood Cliffs, NJ.
  Origin of the **domain criterion**: score an alternative by the *size of the region of the uncertainty space* over which it meets a stated acceptability requirement, rather than by its expected or worst-case performance. The empirical form used throughout the water-resources lineage — the fraction of a finite scenario ensemble in which all criteria hold — is the sample estimator of that region measure, which is why the metric depends on both the criteria *and* the composition of the ensemble that estimates it.

- **The threshold-convention problem.** No one in this lineage *derives* a satisficing threshold. **Zeff et al. (2014)** (WRR 50(6), 4906-4923, doi:10.1002/2013WR015126) elicited them from the Research Triangle utilities, and every later number descends from those by convention, drifting without stated justification: worst-case cost 5% in **Trindade et al. (2017)** becomes 10% in **Gold et al. (2023)**; restriction frequency 20% becomes 10%; Trindade relaxed reliability from 99% to 98.5% because no solution met 99%. The single threshold in the lineage with a genuine external anchor is Gold et al. (2023)'s peak financial cost < 80% of annual volumetric revenue, taken from AWWA bond-covenant limits. Two consequences, both adopted: thresholds are anchored on external legal/physical goalposts wherever one exists (here, the Delaware River Basin Decree/FFMP quantities), and the remainder are **swept**, because **Quinn et al. (2020)** show robustness-rank agreement *across experimental designs* degrades as the satisficing criterion becomes more stringent — a single arbitrary threshold can manufacture or hide a design effect.

### Metric choice changes the decision

- **Giuliani, M. & Castelletti, A. (2016).** Is robustness really robust? How different definitions of robustness impact decision-making under climate change. *Climatic Change*, 135(3-4), 409-424. doi:10.1007/s10584-015-1586-9
  Optimizes Lake Como operating policies (EMODPS, Borg MOEA, RBF policies) across a 28-member climate-change SOW ensemble under five classical robustness metrics spanning an optimism-pessimism spectrum: maximin (Wald), maximax, Hurwicz, minimax regret (Savage), and Laplace. Each metric is the across-scenario aggregation operator **in the search**, yielding distinct Pareto sets. Critically (Fig. 4b-f), policies designed under aggregation Φ_j and then scored under a different aggregation Φ_k are **dominated** by policies correctly designed under Φ_k, in all five panels: search-measure mismatch is a systematic, one-directional penalty rather than a wash. Misdefining the decision-maker's attitude biases simulated decisions and systematically underestimates performance; rankings conflict sharply across metrics. Argues the robustness *definition* should itself be treated as an uncertain factor. For this study it supplies the null that a coverage-distorted search measure must beat.

- **Lempert, R. J. & Collins, M. T. (2007).** Managing the risk of uncertain threshold responses: Comparison of robust, optimum, and precautionary approaches. *Risk Analysis*, 27(4), 1009-1026. doi:10.1111/j.1539-6924.2007.00940.x
  Compares decision frameworks on a lake-eutrophication threshold model under deep uncertainty: optimal expected utility, the precautionary principle, and three robust-decision approaches — trading optimal performance for reduced sensitivity to assumptions, **satisficing** across a wide range of futures, and keeping options open (flexibility). Robustness is evaluated over a landscape of plausible probability distributions, with expected regret (Savage-style) computed across SOW. The three robust approaches converge on similar strategies, offer a quantitative analytic expression of the precautionary principle's intent, and are preferable to the expected-utility optimum when uncertainty is sufficiently deep and the option set sufficiently rich. Foundational comparison of robustness framings (satisficing vs regret vs flexibility vs optimality).

- **Huang, J., Sangiorgio, M., Wu, W., Maier, H. R., Wang, Q. J., Hughes, J. & Castelletti, A. (2025).** Solving the robustness puzzle: The joint impact of optimization approach, robustness metrics, and scenarios on water resources management under deep uncertainty. *Journal of Environmental Management*, 373, 123540. doi:10.1016/j.jenvman.2024.123540
  Systematically isolates the joint influence of three axes — the same axes this study manipulates — using McPhail et al.'s (2018) T1/T2/T3 framework. Axis 1: optimization approach, contrasting a-posteriori "Pareto-optimal-then-robustness-assessment" (PORA) against robust optimization (RO, embedding the robustness metric directly in the search). Axis 2: robustness metric (maximax, maximin, Hurwicz, regret-, percentile-, and moment-based aggregations). Axis 3: the scenario set used during evaluation. Two case studies (upper Adelaide River dam supply; Lake Como). Finds RO yields comparable or improved robustness versus PORA, and that approach, metric, and scenario-set choices interact to change which solution is preferred. Also the clearest published example of a **degenerate** robustness metric: their undesirable-deviations metric is driven toward zero by making performance *uniformly bad* (a water-supply deficit approaching 1.0 scores as "robust" because it is consistently terrible). Hence the rule adopted here: co-report the raw performance distribution alongside every robustness number.
